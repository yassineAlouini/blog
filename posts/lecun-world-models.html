Why a world model, not an LLM

LeCun's thesis (from his 2022 position paper "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence") is that intelligence needs an internal world model: a function predicting how the world evolves — especially as a consequence of actions — so an agent can plan by simulating futures instead of reacting token-by-token.

The central design choice is where you predict:

Generative models (LLMs, video diffusion, pixel-space world models) predict in observation space — the next token or frame in pixels. This wastes capacity on intrinsically unpredictable detail (every leaf, texture, sensor noise).

JEPA predicts in a learned latent space. Encode observations to abstract embeddings and predict the embedding of the future, discarding what is unpredictable.

That single move — predict in latent space, not pixel space — is the entire conceptual backbone.

JEPA & the collapse problem

Given two views of a signal (a context $x$ and a target $y$ — two crops, or observation-at-$t$ and observation-at-$t{+}1$):

  • An encoder $f_\theta$ maps each to embeddings: $s_x = f_\theta(x)$, $s_y = f_\theta(y)$.
  • A predictor $g_\phi$ predicts the target embedding from the context, optionally conditioned on an action $a$: $\hat s_y = g_\phi(s_x, a)$.
  • An energy measures incompatibility: $E(x,y) = \lVert \hat s_y - s_y \rVert^2$.

This is an energy-based model: low energy = "these go together," and in the world-model case the predictor is the dynamics model.

Fig. 1 — JEPA / LeWorldModel data flow. The predictor models latent dynamics conditioned on the action; the prediction energy is computed entirely in embedding space, while SIGReg pulls the embedding distribution toward an isotropic Gaussian to prevent collapse.

The collapse problem

The fatal failure mode: if $f_\theta$ maps everything to a constant, prediction loss is trivially 0 and the energy landscape is flat — useless. Every JEPA's core engineering problem is preventing representational collapse while keeping a simple prediction loss.

SimCLR / contrastiveNegative samples push apart (needs huge batches)

All of these are heuristic: EMA schedules, stop-gradients, multi-term loss weights, teacher–student asymmetry. LeCun's 2025–26 line removes the heuristics and replaces them with a single principled regularizer.

LeJEPA: making it provable

Key theoretical claim: the isotropic Gaussian is the unique embedding distribution that minimizes worst-case downstream prediction risk. If you don't know what downstream task you'll face, the optimal thing your embeddings can do is be distributed as $\mathcal N(0, I)$ — maximally spread, no privileged directions, no collapse.

So instead of ad hoc variance/covariance terms, you directly regularize the embedding distribution toward isotropic Gaussian via SIGReg (Sketched Isotropic Gaussian Regularization):

  1. Testing Gaussianity in dimension $D$ is expensive, so you sketch: draw random unit directions $u_1,\dots,u_K \in \mathbb S^{D-1}$ and project: $p_k^{(i)} = u_k^\top z_i$.
  2. A distribution is isotropic Gaussian iff every 1-D projection is standard Gaussian (Cramér–Wold). So each $\{p_k^{(i)}\}_i$ must look $\mathcal N(0,1)$.
  3. Test each projection against $\mathcal N(0,1)$ with a characteristic-function goodness-of-fit statistic (Epps–Pulley). With empirical CF $\hat\varphi(t) = \tfrac1N\sum_j e^{i t p_j}$ and the normal's $e^{-t^2/2}$, penalize the weighted distance below.
$$ \text{SIGReg} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{u\sim \mathbb S^{D-1}} \int \big|\, \hat\varphi_u(t) - e^{-t^2/2} \,\big|^2 \, w(t)\,dt $$

This gives linear $O(N)$ time/memory — no negatives, no teacher, no stop-gradient, no EMA. The total objective has essentially one hyperparameter:

$$ \mathcal L_{\text{LeJEPA}} = \underbrace{\lVert g_\phi(f_\theta(x)) - \mathrm{sg}[f_\theta(y)]\rVert^2}_{\text{prediction}} \;+\; \lambda\, \underbrace{\text{SIGReg}\big(\{f_\theta(\cdot)\}\big)}_{\text{isotropic-Gaussian regularizer}} $$

PyTorch reconstruction of SIGReg

Caveat: this is a faithful reconstruction from the papers' descriptions, for illustration — not copied from the official repo. For exact details (frequency grid, weighting, direction count) see rbalestr-lab/lejepa.
import torch, torch.nn as nn

def epps_pulley_1d(p, ts, weight):
    # p: (N,) projected, standardized embeddings; compare ECF to N(0,1) CF
    # phi_hat(t) = mean_j exp(i t p_j)
    tp = ts[:, None] * p[None, :]                 # (T, N)
    re = torch.cos(tp).mean(dim=1)                # Re phi_hat(t)
    im = torch.sin(tp).mean(dim=1)               # Im phi_hat(t)
    target = torch.exp(-0.5 * ts**2)             # CF of N(0,1) (real)
    diff2 = (re - target)**2 + im**2             # |phi_hat - phi_gauss|^2
    return (diff2 * weight).sum()                # weighted L2 ~ Epps-Pulley

def sigreg(Z, n_dirs=256, n_freq=64, t_max=5.0):
    # Z: (N, D) batch of embeddings
    N, D = Z.shape
    Z = (Z - Z.mean(0)) / (Z.std(0) + 1e-6)      # center / scale per dim
    U = torch.randn(D, n_dirs, device=Z.device)
    U = U / U.norm(dim=0, keepdim=True)          # random unit directions on sphere
    P = Z @ U                                     # (N, n_dirs) sketched 1-D projections
    P = (P - P.mean(0)) / (P.std(0) + 1e-6)      # standardize each projection
    ts = torch.linspace(0.05, t_max, n_freq, device=Z.device)
    w  = torch.exp(-0.5 * ts**2)                  # Gaussian weighting of frequencies
    return torch.stack([epps_pulley_1d(P[:, k], ts, w)
                        for k in range(n_dirs)]).mean()

def lejepa_step(enc, pred, x, y, action, lam=1.0):
    sx, sy = enc(x), enc(y)
    sy = sy.detach()                              # optional; LeJEPA claims you can drop it
    pred_loss = ((pred(sx, action) - sy)**2).sum(-1).mean()
    reg = sigreg(torch.cat([sx, sy], dim=0))
    return pred_loss + lam * reg

LeWorldModel — the actual world model

This is the paper "leworld model" refers to. Authors: Lucas Maes, Quentin Le Lidec, Damien Scieur, Yann LeCun, Randall Balestriero (Mila / NYU / Samsung SAIL / Brown). It is the first JEPA world model that trains stably end-to-end from raw pixels with only two loss terms — applying the LeJEPA insight to action-conditioned dynamics.

Architecture

  • Encoder $z_t = \mathrm{enc}(o_t)$ — maps a pixel frame to a single ~192-dim token (≈200× fewer than DINO-WM's patch tokens).
  • Predictor $\hat z_{t+1} = \mathrm{pred}(z_t, a_t)$ — latent dynamics conditioned on action $a_t$.
  • ~15M parameters, trainable on a single GPU in a few hours.

Loss — only two terms, one tunable weight

$$ \mathcal L_{\text{LeWM}} = \underbrace{\lVert \mathrm{pred}(z_t, a_t) - z_{t+1}\rVert^2}_{\text{next-embedding prediction}} + \lambda\, \underbrace{\text{SIGReg}(Z)}_{\text{Gaussian latent}} $$

This drops the hyperparameter count from six to one vs. the only prior end-to-end JEPA world model. No EMA, no frozen pre-trained encoder, no auxiliary supervision — collapse is prevented purely by SIGReg.

Planning: Model-Predictive Control in latent space

What makes it a world model rather than just an encoder: roll the predictor forward over candidate action sequences and pick the one whose imagined latent trajectory reaches the goal (CEM-style MPC).

@torch.no_grad()
def plan_mpc(enc, pred, o_now, z_goal, action_dim, horizon=10,
             n_samples=512, iters=4, elite=64):
    # Cross-Entropy Method over action sequences, entirely in latent space
    z = enc(o_now)                                       # (1, d)
    mu  = torch.zeros(horizon, action_dim)
    std = torch.ones(horizon, action_dim)
    for _ in range(iters):
        A = mu + std * torch.randn(n_samples, horizon, action_dim)
        zt = z.expand(n_samples, -1).clone()
        cost = torch.zeros(n_samples)
        for h in range(horizon):
            zt = pred(zt, A[:, h])                       # imagine next latent
            cost += ((zt - z_goal)**2).sum(-1)           # distance-to-goal in latent
        idx = cost.topk(elite, largest=False).indices    # keep best action seqs
        mu, std = A[idx].mean(0), A[idx].std(0) + 1e-4    # refit sampling dist
    return mu[0]                                          # execute first action, replan

Same family as PlaNet / Dreamer MPC-over-imagination, but the dynamics live in a non-generative, SIGReg-regularized latent rather than a reconstructive RSSM.

When does it learn a world model?

The theory paper (arXiv 2605.26379) answers when the recovered latents are the true hidden variables — the deep question for any world model.

Main results

Theorem 5.1 (Linear Identifiability). Under a Gaussian world model, LeJEPA recovers the true latents up to an orthogonal rotation $Q$. The alignment loss obeys $\mathcal L(h) \ge 2(1-\rho)n$, with equality iff $h(z) = Qz$.

Theorem 5.2 (Gaussian Uniqueness). The Gaussian is the unique latent distribution (in the class) giving linear identifiability — the converse that justifies SIGReg's target.

Theorem 5.3 (Graceful degradation). Approximate recovery with error $\sim \delta / (2\rho(1-\rho))$ + regularization error, where $\delta$ is the alignment gap.

Assumptions (the "when")

  • Independence of latent components and their transitions.
  • Stationarity — positive pairs share a marginal distribution.
  • Additive OU noise — transitions follow an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process $z' = \rho z + \sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,\eta$.

Proof core

For Gaussian latents the transition operator's eigenfunctions are Hermite polynomials with eigenvalues $\rho^d$ at degree $d$. Higher-degree (nonlinear) components decay faster, so any nonlinear map strictly reduces cross-correlation: $\mathbb E[h_i(z')h_i(z)] \le \rho$, with equality only for linear $h$. The converse uses Sturm–Liouville theory to show only Gaussians yield affine eigenfunctions.

Honest limitation: the guarantees hold under Gaussian latents + OU dynamics + good exploration, and only for the encoder — not the action-conditioned predictor. So "LeWM provably learns the true world model" is not yet established; it's proven for the representation side under idealized assumptions, with the dynamics side still empirical.

Relation to other work

Direct ancestry (LeCun's own line)

I-JEPA → V-JEPA / V-JEPA 2 → LeJEPA → LeWorldModel. The trajectory is "remove every heuristic": negatives → EMA/stop-grad → a single principled distributional regularizer.

World-model families it contrasts with

WorkRelation
Ha & Schmidhuber, "World Models" (2018)VAE + MDN-RNN dynamics + controller. The original; generative latent.
PlaNet / Dreamer v1–v3RSSM latent dynamics with a reconstruction loss; actor-critic in imagination. LeWM shares latent-MPC but drops reconstruction.
DINO-WM (2024)Closest baseline; uses frozen DINO features. LeWM trains the encoder end-to-end with a ~200× smaller token.

The unifying contrast: predict-in-latent + distributional regularizer (LeCun camp) vs. predict-in-observation-space / generative (LLM + video-diffusion camp). LeJEPA's contribution is turning the latent-prediction camp from a bag of heuristics into something with a uniqueness theorem behind it.

The AMI startup

Startup

AMI Labs (Advanced Machine Intelligence), founded by LeCun after leaving Meta, raised a ~$1.03B seed round (announced ~March 2026) — among the largest AI seed rounds ever. Explicit thesis: build world models, a contrarian bet against LLMs as the path to human-level intelligence.

The papers above (LeWorldModel, "When Does LeJEPA Learn a World Model?", plus LeJEPA from late 2025) are the technical output of LeCun's circle defining what the architecture can provably do and where it still falls short. A wave of related 2026 preprints (Causal-JEPA, Var-JEPA, energy-based JEPA libraries, "Stable Deep RL via Isotropic Gaussian Representations") shows the SIGReg / isotropic-Gaussian idea propagating into RL and object-centric modeling.

Critical read: coverage stresses that the funding-round framing oversells novelty — the theory only guarantees encoder-side recovery under idealized assumptions, and the world-model dynamics side remains empirical.
